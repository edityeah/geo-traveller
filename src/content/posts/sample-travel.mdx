The road to Spiti does not begin in Spiti. It begins about four hundred
kilometres earlier, in the foothills where the air still tastes like the plains
— diesel, dust, and frying oil — and ends somewhere past Kaza where you stop
noticing the altitude because there is nothing else to notice.

## Getting there

There are two ways into Spiti: the long way, through Shimla and Kinnaur, and
the dramatic way, over Rohtang and Kunzum from Manali. The dramatic way is
closed eight months of the year. We took the long way.

> The first night I slept in the back of the car because the only guesthouse
> in Pooh wouldn't open the door past nine. The mountains were close enough
> that I could hear pebbles falling off them.

## What you actually do there

Mostly, nothing. You drink butter tea you don't like. You walk to monasteries
that have been there for nine hundred years and were here before "here" was a
country. You read on a bench in the sun until your eyes hurt from the light.

The towns are small. Tabo has one road. Dhankar has one path. Komic claims to
be the highest village in the world with a motorable road, and you believe it
because there is, demonstrably, nothing higher.

### A list of things I ate

- Thukpa, four nights running
- Apricot jam on toast that tasted of apricot trees
- A momo I'm fairly sure was just dough
- More butter tea, on principle

## Going back down

The way out — over Kunzum, past Chandratal, down through Manali — is the
postcard route. It is also the route that breaks axles. We made it. The car
did not, entirely. The mechanic in Manali laughed and charged us less than
dinner had cost the night before.

I came home with a phone full of photos I haven't looked at since. Maybe I'll
post some.
